## Bottom line

The "agent governance" category Lightwork wants to own is **forming but closing fast**, and the closers are the worst-possible incumbents. Between February and May 2026 three things happened that change the calculus: Gartner published its **first-ever Guardian Agents Market Guide** (25 Feb 2026), predicting *rapid consolidation* as large vendors acquire the startups and that guardian agents will *eliminate ~half of incumbent AI-security systems by 2029 in 70% of orgs* (https://thehackernews.com/2026/03/5-learnings-from-first-ever-gartner.html); **Microsoft shipped Entra Agent ID to GA** and **open-sourced the Agent Governance Toolkit under MIT** — the first toolkit to cover all 10 OWASP agentic risks with sub-millisecond policy enforcement, in Python/Rust/TS/Go/.NET (https://opensource.microsoft.com/blog/2026/04/02/introducing-the-agent-governance-toolkit-open-source-runtime-security-for-ai-agents/); and **OneTrust shipped real-time AI agent oversight + runtime guardrail enforcement** (Mar 2026), partly closing the "filing cabinet, not code" weakness the prior doc leaned on (https://siliconangle.com/2026/03/09/onetrust-expands-platform-real-time-ai-governance-agent-oversight-capabilities/). Lightwork's structural advantages (signed tamper-evident audit, air-gapped self-host, a *capable* swarm) are real, but the generic "register + scope + audit my agents" wedge **was just commoditized by Microsoft for free.** The only defensible move is *narrower and deeper*: **the cryptographically-provable, self-hostable, regulated-evidence runtime** for agents in HIPAA/PCI/EU/air-gapped environments — the one square Microsoft-cloud and SaaS-only GRC structurally cannot stand on.

## Per-competitor teardown

**Microsoft (Entra Agent ID + Purview + Agent Governance Toolkit).** *Does:* agent identity issued from the IdP, lifecycle/sponsorship, Purview data governance, Defender threat protection, and Agent 365 as the control plane ($15/user/mo, or E7 bundle $99/user/mo) — all GA by May 2026 (https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/entra/id-governance/agent-id-governance-overview, https://m365admin.handsontek.net/microsoft-agent-365-becomes-generally-available-ga/). *Strength:* owns the identity *and* the OS *and* the productivity surface where enterprise agents actually run; bundling makes governance a line item, not a purchase. **Where Lightwork loses — totally, today:** Microsoft owns the principal at the IdP layer; the open-sourced Toolkit gives away exactly Lightwork's "runtime policy + capability scoping + OWASP-10 enforcement" wedge for $0 with 5-language SDKs and LangChain/CrewAI hooks. We cannot out-distribute or out-free this. *Where we win:* Microsoft governs agents *inside the Microsoft estate*; Lightwork is the cross-cloud / non-Microsoft / on-prem / air-gapped runtime with portable signed evidence.

**Okta (agentic / non-human identity).** *Does:* Cross App Access (XAA) — an open OAuth/OIDC extension for agent-and-app-to-app authz with broad ISV backing (AWS, Google, Salesforce, Box…); "Okta for AI Agents" governance EA in FY27 Q1 (https://www.okta.com/identity-101/cross-app-access-securing-ai-agent-and-app-to-app-connections/, https://www.okta.com/products/govern-ai-agent-identity/). *Strength:* the neutral, multi-cloud IdP — the natural home for non-human identity if you're *not* all-Microsoft. **Where Lightwork loses:** Okta owns the identity-federation layer (SSO/SCIM) Lightwork *doesn't even have yet* — our #1 code gap is Okta's core product. *Where we win / must partner:* identity ≠ runtime behavior + audit. Lightwork should *consume* Okta/Entra identity, not rebuild it, and own the layer they don't: what the agent *did*, provably.

**OneTrust.** *Does:* the broad privacy/GRC/TPRM platform (~$500M ARR, 14,000 customers, 75% of Fortune 100 **[verify]**), now with AI agent discovery, an AI policy library, and runtime guardrail enforcement (https://www.onetrust.com/solutions/ai-governance/). *Strength:* installed base, 55+ framework content library, the privacy-team buying relationship. **Where Lightwork loses:** brand, distribution, content corpus, and the CPO/GRC budget. *Where we win:* OneTrust is in a **Nov-2025 PE sale process** (Marlin/Vista rumored; last marked $4.5B, a down-round) — distracted, cost-focused, integration-bound (https://secureprivacy.ai/blog/onetrust-private-equity-deal-2026). Its agent governance is *inventory + policy assertion*, not enforced cryptographic runtime state. We win on *depth of proof*, not breadth.

**Vanta / Drata / Secureframe.** *Does:* automated evidence collection for SOC 2/ISO/HIPAA; Vanta is the giant ($4.15B, ~$220M rev, 16,000 customers, AI Agent 2.0 + an AI-governance module) (https://sacra.com/c/vanta/). *Strength:* proved enterprises pay for *automated* evidence; fast self-serve motion; now extending into AI governance and questionnaires. **Where Lightwork loses:** SMB/mid-market distribution, auditor partnerships, and a category they *defined*. *Where we win:* they automate evidence for *infosec certs about the company*, not *runtime governance of autonomous agents*. The compliance-as-agentic-labor play (agent-*run* DSAR/ROPA/evidence) is an offensive flank Vanta's checklist model doesn't reach.

**ServiceNow AI Control Tower.** *Does:* discover/observe/govern/secure/measure AI across the enterprise; GA ~Aug 2026; 30 integrations (AWS/GCP/Azure/SAP/Oracle/Workday), NIST + EU AI Act frameworks, and a deep Microsoft Agent 365 integration (https://newsroom.servicenow.com/press-releases/details/2026/ServiceNow-expands-AI-Control-Tower-to-discover-observe-govern-secure-and-measure-AI-deployed-across-any-system-in-the-enterprise/default.aspx). *Strength:* sits on the enterprise system-of-record + workflow engine the CIO already owns; "control tower" is the exact aggregator framing Lightwork wants. **Where Lightwork loses:** this is the most dangerous *enterprise* competitor — it's the meta-governance console enterprises will default to, riding the existing ServiceNow footprint. *Where we win:* ServiceNow observes/aggregates; it is not itself a sandboxed, signed-audit agent *execution* runtime, and it is a heavy, top-down platform, not a self-hostable runtime.

**Credo AI / Holistic AI / IBM watsonx.governance.** *Does:* model-centric AI governance — inventories, risk assessments, ISO 42001/NIST/EU-AI-Act policy packs; Credo is IBM's OEM content engine and a Gartner Guardian-Agent representative vendor (https://www.credo.ai/blog/credo-ai-and-ibm-empowering-trustworthy-ai-through-oem-collaboration). *Strength:* deep regulatory content + analyst credibility + IBM's enterprise reach. **Where Lightwork loses:** the *content/policy library* and the AI-risk-officer relationship — pure documentation depth we don't have. *Where we win:* these are largely *paperwork/assessment* tools about models; they don't enforce or cryptographically prove *agent runtime* behavior.

**Zenity + Palo Alto Prisma AIRS.** *Does:* AI security — Zenity = AISPM/shadow-agent discovery + detection/response; Prisma AIRS 3.0 (Mar 2026) = full agentic lifecycle from model scan to runtime authorization, from a security-platform incumbent (https://www.paloaltonetworks.com/company/press/2026/palo-alto-networks-secures-agentic-ai-with-prisma-airs-3-0). *Strength:* they own the *security* buyer (CISO) and the "Guardian Agent" framing Gartner just blessed; Palo Alto has platform gravity + capital. **Where Lightwork loses:** the security budget, threat-intel depth, and CISO trust. *Where we win:* security ≠ governance/compliance evidence; they sell *protection*, not the tamper-evident, framework-mapped *audit artifact* a regulator/auditor accepts.

## Where we lose

Today, brutally: **distribution** (Microsoft bundle, Okta IdP, ServiceNow footprint, Vanta self-serve — we have none); **trust/brand** (zero name recognition; competitors have F100 logos, analyst placement, public valuations); **certs** (no SOC 2 Type II / ISO 42001 / HIPAA BAA / FedRAMP — table stakes we lack); **content** (no 55-framework corpus; OneTrust/Credo/ServiceNow have it); **capital** (Vanta $4.15B, OneTrust $4.5B, Palo Alto/Microsoft effectively unbounded; Lightwork is an OSS repo); **identity** (Okta/Entra own the layer we haven't built); and **the wedge itself** (Microsoft open-sourced "runtime agent governance" for free in April 2026). We also lose on **product completeness**: no SSO/SCIM, no encryption-at-rest, unfinished multi-tenancy — exactly the regulated-buyer table stakes.

## What would kill us

1. **Picking the generic "agent registry + runtime governance" fight.** Microsoft already gave it away (Agent Governance Toolkit, MIT, sub-ms, 5 languages, OWASP-10). Competing there is competing with free, from the OS vendor. **This is the single most likely killer.**
2. **Fighting at the identity layer.** Building an IdP to rival Okta/Entra is a multi-hundred-million-dollar war we lose by default. Consume their identity; never compete with it.
3. **Cloning OneTrust's breadth** (CMP, 55-framework content, TPRM) — a content/GTM slog against a deep, capitalized, soon-PE-owned incumbent.
4. **Moving too slowly.** Gartner forecasts *rapid consolidation* and obsolescence of half of today's AI-security tooling by 2029. A 2027–2028 cert/GTM timeline misses the window; the consolidation closes around the platform vendors first.

## Recommendations

- **Pick the one defensible wedge: the *provable, self-hostable, regulated* agent runtime.** Not "govern agents" (commoditized) but "run agents on PHI/PCI/EU/classified data with cryptographically tamper-evident, framework-mapped, exportable evidence, air-gapped, no Microsoft cloud required." This is the square Microsoft (cloud/Windows-bound), Okta (identity-only), ServiceNow (SaaS aggregator), and Vanta (company-cert) structurally cannot occupy. Lightwork already *is* a capable swarm with Ed25519 signed audit + 7 sandboxes — defensible because it's our existing substrate, not paperwork.
- **Consume identity, don't build it.** Integrate Entra Agent ID + Okta XAA as the principal source; map onto the existing `Capability.principal`/tenant model. Turns the two scariest competitors into upstream dependencies.
- **Lead with the evidence artifact, against OneTrust's distraction window.** While OneTrust is in its PE sale, position the signed audit chain → auto-populated EU AI Act Art. 12/14 + SR 11-7 + SOC 2 evidence as the thing their inventory can't produce. Partner for content; don't clone it.
- **Compress the timeline.** Pull SSO/SCIM, encryption-at-rest, finished tenancy, and SOC 2 Type II into one near-term "Governed Runtime" track. The category window is open *now* and Gartner says it's closing — ship the regulated-runtime proof in 2026, not 2028.
- **Concede where conceding is correct.** Don't fight Microsoft on free runtime governance, Okta on identity, or OneTrust on content breadth. Win on *provable + self-hostable + regulated*, the intersection none of them owns.
