# What this skill does

Designs an influencer/creator marketing program: audience-fit criteria, a vetted shortlist of creator profiles, partnership structure and briefs, and a measurement framework with target KPIs. Produces an executable plan that matches creators to a real audience and message, with measurement defined up front so the program is accountable rather than vibes-based.

# Steps

1. Anchor the program with `knowledge_search` over internal context: target persona, product message, budget range, channels in scope, and any past creator results. State the primary objective (awareness, consideration, conversion) and the budget assumption explicitly.
2. Build selection criteria, then source candidate creators with `web_search` against those criteria (platform, niche relevance, audience overlap, engagement quality, brand-safety signals). Cite each candidate's source/handle; mark follower and engagement figures as unverified unless confirmed by a first-party platform or analytics tool — never fabricate metrics.
3. Define partnership structure: tier mix (macro vs. mid vs. micro), compensation model (flat, affiliate, gifting, hybrid), deliverables and usage rights, and disclosure requirements (FTC/ASA-style sponsored labeling). Write a creator brief template covering message, do/don't list, mandatory claims-substantiation, and approval checkpoints.
4. Specify measurement: per-objective KPIs (reach, engagement rate, trackable links/codes, attributed conversions), a baseline, and a review cadence. Report the full plan with the shortlist, briefs, and measurement framework; list assumptions and stage all creator outreach and contracts for human approval before any commitment.

# Notes

The plan is wrong if follower/engagement numbers are taken at face value (bot inflation, bought followers) or presented without a source — always mark unverified and recommend a first-party audit before paying. Influencer-attributed conversions are noisy; use unique codes/links and treat lift as directional. Disclosure and claims substantiation are legal requirements, not options — the brief must enforce them. This produces a draft program; a human signs contracts, approves spend, and owns any binding outreach. Do not use for regulated-product endorsements without legal review, or when no creator-suitable audience exists for the product.
