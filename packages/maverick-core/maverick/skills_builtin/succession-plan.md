# What this skill does

Prepares for continuity of key roles by mapping incumbents to potential successors and assessing the depth and readiness of the bench. Produces a succession plan that flags roles at risk (no ready successor, single point of failure) and quantifies bench depth and successor readiness per critical role. Handles annual talent reviews, reorganizations, and pre-departure contingency planning.

# Steps

1. Establish the scope from real inputs: the critical/key roles in question, their current incumbents, and any flight-risk or retirement signals. Pull the organization's readiness framework (e.g. ready-now / ready 1-2 yrs / ready 3+ yrs definitions) via knowledge_search and cite it; if none exists, state the rubric you are applying.
2. For each key role, identify candidate successors from available talent data and rate each on readiness against the cited rubric, noting the evidence (performance, scope, gaps). Do not invent successors or readiness levels — where data is absent, mark the role as having an unverified or empty bench.
3. In a spreadsheet, compute bench depth per role (count of ready-now plus ready-soon successors) and tag risk roles: zero ready successors, single-candidate dependency, or successor who is themselves a key incumbent (cascade risk). Roll up to an org-level view of where continuity is thin.
4. Report the succession plan with the per-role bench table, readiness ratings, and the prioritized list of risk roles plus recommended development or backfill actions. State assumptions and data gaps; present as recommendations for HR/leadership review — do not communicate successor designations to individuals.

# Notes

The output is wrong if a role with only an unproven or not-yet-ready candidate is shown as covered — bench depth counts ready successors, not names on a list. Readiness ratings are judgments; tie each to evidence and mark thin assessments as low-confidence. Watch cascade risk: naming a key incumbent as another role's successor does not add real depth. Do not use this to make promotion or termination decisions, and never surface successor designations to employees — these are sensitive drafts staged for a human leadership decision.
