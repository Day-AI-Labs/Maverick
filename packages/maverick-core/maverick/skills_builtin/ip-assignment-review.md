# What this skill does

Reviews the intellectual-property assignment and ownership terms of a contract to confirm clean title transfer and to flag gaps that leave ownership ambiguous. Produces a review covering the assignment grant, "work made for hire" language, present-vs-future assignment, background/pre-existing IP carve-outs, moral-rights waivers, further-assurances, and any retained license-back. Output is a draft review with identified gaps and recommended carve-outs; a human/counsel approves before negotiation.

# Steps

1. Locate the terms: knowledge_search for "Assignment," "Ownership of Work Product," "Work Made for Hire," "Intellectual Property," "Background IP," "Pre-Existing Materials," "License," and "Moral Rights." Quote each clause verbatim with its section number; mark any absent clause as MISSING.
2. Verify the grant mechanics: confirm the assignment is a present grant ("hereby assigns") not a mere agreement to assign in future, that it covers all IP types (copyright, patent, trade secret), and that "work made for hire" is backed by a present assignment fallback (the WFH doctrine does not reach all deliverables or jurisdictions). Flag any future-tense or conditional assignment as a title gap.
3. Check the carve-outs and retained rights: identify background/pre-existing IP and third-party/open-source materials, confirm the deliverable owner receives a license to anything not assigned, and check for a license-back to the assignor. Note any over-broad assignment that sweeps in unrelated or pre-existing IP the assignor should retain.
4. Assess perfection terms: confirm further-assurances, power-of-attorney for filings, and a moral-rights waiver (where waivable). Then report the review as a gap list (each gap tied to its clause and risk) plus recommended carve-outs and redline language, stating assumptions and noting that binding edits require human/counsel sign-off.

# Notes

Output is wrong if it accepts an "agrees to assign" as a completed present assignment, relies on "work for hire" alone without an assignment fallback, or misses that background IP was assigned away or, conversely, that no license-back was granted (leaving the recipient unable to use delivered work). Always quote the source clause and section; never infer ownership from memory. This is a draft/recommend skill — it stages gaps and carve-out language for a reviewer or counsel who decides the negotiating position. Do not use it for agreements with no IP deliverable, or as a substitute for a jurisdiction-specific opinion on assignability and moral-rights waivability.
