# What this skill does

Stress-tests a strategy against divergent futures by isolating the two most critical, most uncertain driving forces and crossing them into a 2x2 matrix of four internally-consistent scenarios. Produces a named scenario set, early-warning signposts for each, and the no-regret moves that pay off across all four — so a plan is robust to uncertainty rather than tuned to a single forecast.

# Steps

1. Frame the decision and horizon, then knowledge_search the driving forces (market, regulatory, technological, competitive) bearing on it. Rank each force by impact AND uncertainty; cite the basis for each ranking and mark speculative forces unverified.
2. Pick the two forces that are both high-impact and high-uncertainty as the matrix axes (forces that are high-impact but near-certain become baseline assumptions, not axes). State why these two, not others.
3. Build the 2x2 in the spreadsheet: for each of the four quadrants write a short, internally-consistent narrative — a future where both axis conditions hold together — and give it a memorable name. Reject any quadrant that is logically incoherent and note why.
4. For each scenario, list 2-3 observable signposts (leading indicators that this future is arriving) and the strategic implications. Then identify no-regret moves (robust across all four) and contingent bets (quadrant-specific). Hand off the matrix, signposts, and move list, stating which forces were assumed.

# Notes

Scenarios are not forecasts and carry no probabilities — presenting one quadrant as "most likely" defeats the method and invites planning to a single guess. The classic failures: axes that are correlated (collapsing the 2x2 into a diagonal), or four scenarios that are really one optimistic/pessimistic spread — both mean the axes weren't truly independent and uncertain. Garbage driving-force research yields plausible-sounding but hollow scenarios, so ground forces in cited evidence. This skill recommends moves and watch-items; committing capital to a contingent bet is a human decision. Don't use it when the future is genuinely low-uncertainty — a forecast or sensitivity analysis is the right tool there.
