# What this skill does

Reviews a single conflict-of-interest disclosure (financial interest, outside activity, related-party relationship, gift, or independence concern) against the organization's COI policy and produces a draft assessment: whether a conflict exists, its materiality, and a recommended mitigation or disposition. Output supports an ethics/compliance officer or committee decision; it does not clear or prohibit the activity on its own.

# Steps

1. Extract the disclosed facts from the submission: the discloser's role and decision authority, the nature and size of the interest/relationship, the counterparty, and the specific organizational decision or transaction it could touch. Record dates and dollar amounts as stated; mark anything missing (e.g., ownership percentage, family member's role) as a gap to confirm.
2. Use `knowledge_search` to pull the governing COI policy, code of conduct, and any role-specific rules (procurement, research, board, fiduciary). Cite the specific policy clauses that define a reportable conflict and the materiality thresholds; note where the policy is silent and judgment is required.
3. Assess existence and materiality: map the disclosed interest to the decisions the discloser influences, judge directness (actual / potential / perceived) and magnitude against policy thresholds, and identify who else is implicated. State the materiality rationale explicitly rather than asserting a conclusion.
4. Recommend a disposition from the policy's mitigation menu (no conflict / manage via recusal / disclosure-and-monitor / divestiture / prohibition / escalate to committee), tied to the materiality finding. Report the draft review with cited clauses, list unresolved facts, and hand off to the ethics owner or committee for the binding determination.

# Notes

Materiality is the crux and the most common error: under- or over-calling it both cause harm, so anchor every materiality call to a cited policy threshold or an explicit, stated rationale — never a gut feel. Perceived conflicts can require mitigation even when no actual bias exists; don't dismiss them. The review reflects only the facts disclosed — an incomplete or self-serving disclosure produces an unreliable result, so flag gaps prominently. This skill drafts and recommends; recusal orders, prohibitions, divestiture demands, and clearances are decisions for the designated official or committee. Do not use for legal conflict-of-interest waivers requiring counsel, or for systemic/program-level COI audits.
