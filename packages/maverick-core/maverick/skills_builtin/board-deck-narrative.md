# What this skill does

Structures an executive or board presentation around a clear narrative arc rather than a pile of slides. Produces a deck outline: a storyline (situation, complication, ask, evidence, plan) mapped to a slide sequence, each slide carrying one headline takeaway. The output is a skeleton a leader fills with final visuals.

# Steps

1. Establish purpose and audience from real inputs — the meeting agenda, prior board decks, the decision or approval being sought. Use `knowledge_search` to retrieve actuals, KPIs, and prior commitments; cite each. If the metrics behind a claim are missing, flag it rather than asserting a number.
2. Define the narrative arc first: where we are, what changed / what is at stake, the recommendation or ask, the evidence, and the forward plan. Keep it to one through-line a board member could repeat in a sentence.
3. Map the arc to a slide sequence. For each slide give a one-line headline takeaway (the assertion, not the topic), the 2-3 supporting points, and the data/exhibit it needs. Front-load the ask; relegate detail to an appendix section.
4. Deliver the outline plus a one-paragraph executive summary and an appendix list. State assumptions about audience and decision, and mark any figure not traced to a sourced metric as UNVERIFIED so it gets confirmed before the meeting.

# Notes

The deck is wrong if takeaways are topics ("Q3 results") instead of assertions ("Q3 beat plan; we should reinvest in X"), or if numbers are unsourced — a board will catch a fabricated metric and lose trust. Overlong decks with no arc are the usual failure. This skill drafts the structure and narrative; it does not approve the ask or commit the company to anything — the presenter and board decide. Do not use it to invent financials or to replace finance/legal review of stated figures.
