# What this skill does

Maps the competitors in a defined market onto a positioning view and identifies white space the subject company could occupy. Produces a competitor inventory with comparable attributes, a two-axis positioning map, and a short list of underserved gaps. Every competitor attribute is sourced; the map reflects evidence, not impression.

# Steps

1. Define the market and the lens: the category, the buyer segment, and the two axes that actually differentiate players here (e.g. price vs breadth, self-serve vs enterprise). State the axes you chose and why.
2. Build the competitor inventory using `web_search` for public players and `knowledge_search` for internal CRM/win-loss/prior maps; capture each competitor's segment, positioning, pricing posture, and a distinguishing strength, with a source per row. Mark gaps as `[unverified]`.
3. Cluster competitors by strategy and plot them on the chosen two axes; note where players bunch (red ocean) and where the map is sparse.
4. Identify white space: sparse regions backed by real but underserved demand, distinguished from sparse-because-unviable regions — say which is which and on what evidence.
5. Report: deliver the competitor table, the positioning map (axes + plotted players), and the ranked white-space opportunities with the subject's fit to each. Restate the axis choice and list `[unverified]` rows.

# Notes

The map is wrong when axes are vanity dimensions that don't separate players, when "white space" is just an unviable corner with no demand, or when competitor attributes are guessed rather than sourced. This is an analytical input that recommends where to look, not a directive to reposition — a human owns that call. Do not use for a full strategic posture (use swot-and-porters) or to decide whether to enter a market at all (use market-entry-analysis); requires a defined market to map.
