# What this skill does

Crafts a product positioning statement for a specific target segment: names the market
category, states the differentiated value, and backs each claim with proof. Produces a
single tight statement plus the supporting proof points, grounded in real product and
market evidence rather than aspiration.

# Steps

1. Pull the inputs with `knowledge_search`: target customer/segment, the top jobs or
   pains they have, current alternatives they use, the product's actual capabilities,
   and any win/loss or competitive notes. Note what you could not find as unverified.
2. Choose the market category — the frame of reference the buyer already shops in — and
   confirm the product credibly belongs in it. Avoid inventing a new category unless the
   evidence shows the existing one actively misleads the buyer.
3. Draft the statement in the form: "For [target] who [need], [product] is a [category]
   that [key differentiated benefit], unlike [primary alternative], because [reason to
   believe]." Tie the differentiator to a capability the product has today, not roadmap.
4. Attach 2-4 proof points (metrics, customer evidence, capability facts), each cited to
   its source; mark anything unverified. Report the statement plus proof and state the
   assumptions (target, primary alternative) so a marketing owner can validate them.

# Notes

The statement is wrong if the differentiator is generic ("easy to use", "AI-powered")
or if it relies on roadmap rather than shipped capability — buyers discount both.
Picking the wrong category is the most expensive error: it sets the comparison set and
the budget line, so verify it against how the segment actually buys. Every proof point
must cite a source; do not fabricate metrics or logos. This drafts positioning for human
review — it does not approve messaging, pricing, or category bets. Do not use it to write
campaign copy or a full launch narrative (those build on the approved statement).
