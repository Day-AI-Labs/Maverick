# What this skill does

Equips sales reps to win a deal against a named rival by producing a one-page battlecard: head-to-head positioning, where we win and where they win, landmines to set, and rebuttals to the objections that rival's reps raise. Grounds claims in our own product knowledge and the competitor's public material, not assertion.

# Steps

1. Confirm the target rival, our product/segment, and the deal context (buyer persona, common objections heard). Pull our positioning, pricing posture, and proof points from internal sources via `knowledge_search`; never invent feature claims about our own product.
2. Research the competitor with `web_search`: their public positioning, pricing, recent releases, analyst/review-site sentiment (G2, Gartner), and known weaknesses. Cite each external claim with its source and date; mark anything thin or unconfirmed as "unverified".
3. Build the head-to-head: 4-6 dimensions buyers actually decide on, with an honest where-we-win / where-they-win call per dimension. Derive 2-3 "traps" (discovery questions that expose their weakness) and an objection-handling table mapping each rebuttal to a proof point.
4. Assemble the one-page battlecard (positioning statement, win/loss grid, traps, objection rebuttals) and report it, stating which claims are sourced vs. inferred and flagging any competitive claim that legal/marketing should vet before external use.

# Notes

The card is wrong — and dangerous — if it overstates our strengths or makes disparaging claims about the competitor that aren't substantiated; those create legal and credibility risk, so keep claims defensible and sourced. Public pricing and feature info goes stale fast; date every external fact. Do not let reps make unvetted public-facing competitive claims — stage anything externally quotable for human marketing/legal review. Not for full market-sizing or win/loss program analysis.
