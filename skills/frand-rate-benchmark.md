# What this skill does

This skill assembles the analytical inputs for a FRAND (fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory) royalty position on standard-essential patents: a set of comparable license benchmarks, an essentiality-screening worksheet, and the framing for a top-down or comparable-license rate analysis. It gathers and organizes evidence — public comparable rates, pool rates, prior determinations, patent-counting inputs — but the actual FRAND rate is a legal/economic conclusion owned by the attorney and testifying expert; this skill never states "the rate is X." The output is a benchmark workbook and essentiality screen, staged for counsel.

# Steps

1. Use knowledge_search and web_search to gather comparable-license evidence: publicly disclosed bilateral rates, patent-pool royalty schedules for the relevant standard, prior court/arbitral FRAND determinations, and any company-internal comparable licenses, recording the source and the comparability factors (same standard, similar field of use, similar scope, arms-length) for each.
2. Build the essentiality-screening worksheet: for each asserted patent, capture the claimed standard section, a preliminary essential/likely/not-clear screen, and the basis — flagging that a rigorous essentiality determination is claim-chart work for an expert, so the screen is a triage, not a ruling.
3. Frame the rate analysis inputs both ways: top-down (aggregate royalty burden for the standard × the portfolio's share of essential patents) and comparable-license (adjusted for the comparability factors), and lay out the non-discrimination check (are similarly situated licensees offered similar terms?). Note where data is missing or weak.
4. Assemble the benchmark workbook (comparables table with sources and comparability notes, essentiality screen, top-down and comparable inputs, non-discrimination notes, data gaps) and stage it for counsel. Mark clearly that the FRAND rate itself is an attorney/expert conclusion — this skill provides the inputs, not the number.

# Notes

The rate is attorney-and-expert owned: provide benchmarks, ranges, and inputs, but do not assert a FRAND rate — that conclusion carries legal and litigation consequences. Comparability is everything in comparable-license analysis: a rate from a different standard, field of use, or non-arms-length deal is not a true comparable — annotate the comparability factors so counsel can weigh each, and don't average incomparable rates into a single figure. Essentiality here is a triage screen, not a determination; real essentiality needs claim charts mapped to the standard by an expert — say so. Top-down and comparable-license approaches can diverge widely; present both with their assumptions rather than reconciling them yourself. This skill assembles evidence and stages it; it makes no licensing offer or rate commitment.
